Evaluating Company Financials: Balance Sheet

What is a balance sheet

The balance sheet presents a snapshot of a company’s financial position at a specific point in time. Key components include assets, liabilities, and shareholders’ equity. Analysts analyze total assets and debt levels to assess a company’s financial stability and leverage. A company’s assets are the sum of the liabilities and shareholder’s equity.

First, let’s define the key terms:

  • Assets - Everything owned by the company
  • Liabilites - Financial obligations or debts that a company owes to external parties
  • Shareholder’s equity - The company’s value after deducting all liabilities from its assets. It’s what remains for the shareholders once all debts are paid or settled.

What is a balance sheet

Assets

Current Assets:

Current assets are a category of assets on a company’s balance sheet that are expected to be converted into cash or used up within one year or the operating cycle, whichever is longer. They are important indicators of a company’s short-term liquidity and ability to meet its current obligations. There are many types of current assets, below listed are the main three.

Cash and cash equivalents:

This includes cash in hand and cash in bank accounts, as well as highly liquid investments that are easily convertible to known amounts of cash and have a short maturity period, usually within three months.

Accounts receivable:

These are amounts owed to the company by its customers for goods sold or services rendered on credit. It represents the money that the company is yet to receive from its customers.

Inventory:

Inventory refers to the goods and materials held by the company for the purpose of resale or production. It includes raw materials, work-in-progress, and finished goods.

Prepaid expenses:

Prepaid expenses are expenses paid in advance but not yet incurred. They represent future expenses that have been paid for in the current period. Common examples include prepaid insurance, prepaid rent, or prepaid subscriptions.

Non-Current Assets:

Non-current assets are long-term assets that are not expected to be converted into cash or used up within one year or the operating cycle. They represent the long-term investments and resources of the company. There are many types of non-current assets, below listed are the main three.

Fixed assets:

Fixed assets, also known as property, plant, and equipment (PP&E), are tangible assets that have a long useful life and are used in the production or supply of goods and services. Examples include buildings, machinery, vehicles, and furniture.

Long-term investments:

Long-term investments are investments in other companies or financial instruments that the company intends to hold for an extended period, typically longer than one year. Examples include investments in stocks, bonds, and long-term loans to other companies.

Intangible assets:

Intangible assets are assets that lack physical substance but have value to the company. They represent rights or privileges that provide future economic benefits. Examples include patents, trademarks, copyrights, goodwill, and intellectual property.

Liabilities

Current Liabilities

Current liabilities are obligations or debts that a company is expected to settle within one year or the operating cycle, whichever is longer. They represent the company’s short-term financial obligations that need to be paid off in the near future. There are many types of current liabilities, below listed are the main three.

Accounts payable:

Accounts payable are amounts owed by the company to its suppliers or vendors for goods or services received on credit. It represents the company’s short-term trade payables.

Accrued expenses:

Accrued expenses are expenses that the company has incurred but has not yet paid for. They represent the company’s liabilities for expenses that have been recognized but not yet settled, such as salaries, utilities, or taxes.

Short-term debt:

Short-term debt, also known as the current portion of long-term debt, is the portion of long-term debt that is due to be repaid within the next year. It includes loans, notes, or other borrowings that are classified as current liabilities because they are expected to be settled in the short term.

Non-current Liabilities:

Non-current liabilities are long-term obligations that are not expected to be settled within one year or the operating cycle. They represent the company’s long-term financial obligations that extend beyond the current year.

Long-term debt:

Long-term debt is the portion of debt that is due to be repaid over a period longer than one year. It includes loans, bonds, mortgages, and other borrowings with maturity dates beyond the next twelve months.

Deferred revenue:

Deferred revenue, also known as unearned revenue, represents payments received in advance from customers for goods or services that are yet to be delivered or performed. The revenue is recognized as the goods or services provided.

Pension liabilities:

Pension liabilities represent the company’s obligation to provide pension benefits to its employees after their retirement. It includes the promised payments to employees based on their years of service and salary levels.

Long-term liabilites:

This is a general category that includes other long-term financial obligations that a company may have, such as long-term lease obligations, deferred tax liabilities, and other long-term contractual obligations.

Shareholder’s equity

Shareholder’s equity, also known as stockholders’ equity or owners’ equity, represents the residual interest in the assets of a company after deducting its liabilities. It is the value that would be left for shareholders if all the company’s debts and obligations were settled.

Shareholder’s equity reflects the company’s net worth and represents the ownership interest of the shareholders in the company. The components of shareholder’s equity below are the different parts that make up the total value of shareholder’s equity. There are many components to shareholder’s equity, below listed are the main three.

Common Stock:

Common stock represents the ownership shares issued to shareholders, and it entitles them to voting rights and a portion of the company’s profits through dividends. It is the most common form of equity and holds the highest claim on the company’s assets in case of liquidation.

Additional Paid-In Capital:

This represents the amount shareholders have paid above the par value of the shares. It includes the excess amounts paid by investors over the face value of the stock during the initial public offering (IPO) or subsequent offerings.

Common Stock:

Retained earnings are the accumulated profits of the company that have been reinvested back into the business rather than distributed as dividends. It represents the company’s past earnings and reflects its ability to generate profits over time.
