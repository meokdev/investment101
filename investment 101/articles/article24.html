Evaluating Company Financials: Cash Flow Statement

What is a Cash Flow Statement

The cash flow statement tracks the inflow and outflow of cash from operating, investing, and financing activities. Analysts examine metrics such as operating cash flow and free cash flow to assess a company’s ability to generate and manage cash.

Cash flow is how much money the company has at hand at the current moment. It is commonly confused with revenue, but they are not the same thing. A company can have positive cash flow and not be profitable, and vice versa.

This is because companies can allow consumers to buy their products in installments. This means that they won’t receive the payment in full yet at that current moment. Their revenue will still be the full payment, but their cash flow will only be a segment of the full payment. Similarly, a company can buy materials needed for production in installments. Their cash flow will only decrease at that moment by a fraction of the decrease in revenue.

Non-cash charges

Non-cash charges are accounting entries in a company’s financial statements that represent expenses incurred but do not require a direct cash payment. Despite not involving actual cash outflows, these charges play a significant role in shaping the company’s financial performance and overall profitability. They are essential to consider because they provide a more comprehensive view of the company’s financial health beyond just the immediate cash transactions. Because these expenses do not involve cash payments, they are added to the net income to get net cash flow as there isn’t real cash spending.

Stock based compensation:

Stock-based compensation is a type of benefit to allow employees to purchase their company stock at a discount price or for free. The cost of this is typically included in the income statement. However, this expense doesn’t involve an actual cash outflow. The company is not handing out cash to employees; it’s giving them shares of its own stock. This has a negative impact on the company’s value, but in terms of cash flow, it’s not money going out the door. It’s a way to compensate employees by giving them a piece of the company’s ownership. While it reduces the net income, it is also added back to the cash flow statement.

Depreciation & Amortization:

Depreciation and amortization are non-cash expenses incurred by a company for long-term assets. This is when the asset’s value reduces over time. Depreciation is the decrease in the value of tangible assets such as buildings, machinery, and equipment. Amortization is the reduction of the value of intangible assets such as patents and copyrights. They allocate the cost of assets over their useful life. In the income statement, these expenses are spread across COGS and operating expenses. The cash flow statement shows the total amount of depreciation and amortization for the period.

Changes in working capital:

Changes in working capital refer to the net increase or decrease in a company’s current assets and liabilities over a specific period. It reflects the fluctuation in funds available for day-to-day operations. Positive changes indicate increased liquidity, while negative changes suggest decreased availability of funds. Factors such as inventory levels, accounts receivable, accounts payable, and short-term borrowings can impact working capital.

Increase in inventory:

If a company increases its inventory levels, it may require more funds to purchase and store the additional inventory, leading to a decrease in working capital.

Decrease in accounts receivable:

If a company collects payments from customers more quickly, it can convert accounts receivable into cash, resulting in an increase in working capital.

Increase in accounts payable:

Delaying payments to suppliers can provide a short-term increase in working capital as the company retains funds for other purposes.

Investing activities

Investing activities are one of the three main sections of the cash flow statement. This section provides insights into the company’s cash flows related to its investments in various assets over a specific period. Investing activities include both cash inflows and outflows related to acquiring and disposing of long-term assets.

Changes in property, plant, and equipment (PP&E):

These represent long-term assets used by the company to generate revenue. Increased purchases of PP&E can indicate positive investment in future growth, while high levels of sales may raise concerns about asset divestment. However, increased purchases of PP&E also mean there’s less cash liquidity, and vice versa. Context is crucial in interpreting these changes.

Changes in investments:

These refer to the company’s investments in other companies or assets aimed at generating additional income. An increase in investments may imply that the company is looking to expand its asset portfolio, potentially seeking higher returns, while a decrease in investments may imply that the company is trying to reduce its exposure to certain assets, possibly to free up cash or adjust its risk profile. Monitoring changes in investment levels provides insights into the company’s investment strategies and potential impact on its financial performance.

Financing activities

Financing Activities is a section of the cash flow statement that provides insights into the company’s cash flows related to its financing and capital structure. This section reflects the company’s interactions with its investors and creditors, including activities that involve issuing or repurchasing equity and debt, as well as distributing profits to shareholders.

Changes in debt issuance or repayment:

Companies have the option to borrow money from lenders or repay existing debt. Issuance of debt means that there is an inflow of cash from borrowing money from lenders.

This may mean that a company is raising capital to fund its operation, potentially increasing its revenue in the long run. However, too much debt could also indicate increased financial risk.

Repayment of debt shows the outflow of cash from repaying the principal amount of previously borrowed funds. This demonstrates the company’s commitment to managing its debt obligations, which can enhance its creditworthiness and financial stability.

Monitoring these changes is important to assess if the company is taking on excessive debt, which requires a thorough review of the balance sheet.

Changes in equity issuance or repurchase:

Companies can issue new shares to raise funds or buy back their own shares.

Issuing of equity represents the cash inflow from issuing new shares of stock. Issuing equity allows the company to raise capital without causing debt. However, it dilutes ownership as new shareholders enter the picture.

Repurchase of equity consumes cash but it can indicate company confidence and can also boost the value per share for existing shareholders.

Dividend payments:

Companies often distribute a portion of their profits to shareholders as dividends, serving as a reward for their investment in the company. Dividend distribution can attract potential investors. The level of dividends paid can reflect the company’s financial health and its commitment to returning value to its shareholders. Monitoring dividend payments helps investors assess the company’s dedication to providing returns to its shareholders.

In conclusion, it’s important to avoid the assumption that high cash flow always equates to a company’s strong financial health. While a substantial cash flow might seem positive at first glance, a closer examination of its components reveals a more nuanced picture. For instance, elevated cash flow could be driven by a company’s desperate efforts to sell off assets or push products aggressively due to underlying challenges. Similarly, focusing solely on cash flow without considering factors like investment strategies or overall financial stability could lead to incomplete assessments. Therefore, a comprehensive analysis that considers various aspects of cash flow is essential to make informed decisions about a company’s financial well-being.
